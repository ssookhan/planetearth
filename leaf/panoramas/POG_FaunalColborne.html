Where am I?
You are at the Port Colborne Quarry, also known as the Wainfleet Conservation Area. This site is located just north of Lake Erie near the Welland Canal.
Rugose Corals
The white circle shapes with interal radiating spokes is a cross cection through a rugose coral.
A pleasant walk
The quarry is now a conservation area where one can enjoy a nice quite stroll through a naturalized wetland environment while spotting fossils.
What are these rocks?
The layers in this quarry face are beds of limestone. Each bed represents either a period of growth of a coral reef, or a period of deposition of detritus derived from a coral reef.
Bryozoans and crinoids
This photo shows branching bryosoan fossils and broken up crinoids (which are a type of echinoderm).
What's Missing?
The following fossils have never been found here: trilobites, ammonites, birds, dinosaurs, mammals, plants, pollen, bivalves (like clams or mussels), trees, birds, and stromatolites (fossilized algal mat). Some of these may have existed while the limestone was forming, but simply have lived in a different environment. Others were not living at that time altogether.
The Tale of the Quarry
This was once a cement quarry where the limestone rock was crushed into aggregate for use in concrete. It has since won awards for efforts to rehabilitate the site back to a natural condition.
Crinoids
The white hollow rectangle shapes are crinoid stems. Crinoids are flower-like echinoderm, or sea lilly.
The Assemblage.
Most of the fossils found here are animals that lived in a warm, tropical seaway. The following categories have been observed: Gastropods (sea snails), Bryozoa (moss animals), Echinoderms (crinoids, sea lillies), Brachiopods, Favosites corals, Stauriida corals, and Stromatoporoids. There are even rare armours plating from the primitive fish.
