The Escarpment
The Niagara Escarpent is a series of cliffs that stretches over 1000 km from Rochester, New York to Green Bay, Wisconsin. The Ontario portion is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve.
Irondequoit Formation
This formation is a limestone made out of fragments of hard-bodied or shelled organisms like crinoids. The Irondequoit is a hard unit and often forms a natural ledge or shelf.
Rusting Rocks
Some rocks here are stained reddish from oxidized iron in the groundwater.
Thorold Formation Sandstones
The Thorold Formation was deposited in a sandy beach-like setting.
Skyway Bridge
The first steel-structure bridge crossing the Burlington Bay Canal was completed in 1958 and twinned in 1985. Before this, a lift bridge operated along Hamilton Beach, which meant traffic between Toronto and Buffalo was often delayed. The Skybridge, heralded as Canadas first aerial expressway, allows for uninterupted vehicle passage as well as shipping passage into Hamilton Harbour.
Lockport Formation
The Silurian Lockport Formation is the primary caprock of the Niagara Escarpment. It is made out of a tough resistant dolomite, formed in a warm shallow sea out of the degraded remains of shelled animals. It is the youngest rock layer seen at this site.
An Area of Concern
An Area of Concern is a region in the Great Lakes that has experienced a high level of environmental damage from human activity. Hamilton Harbour contains waste products from over a century of steel smelting as well as the discharge from three wastewater treatments plants, and urban runoff from the cities of Hamilton and Burlington. Sediments are contaminated by metals, polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs).
What's in a name?
The Jolley Cut is named after saddler, harness maker and politician James Jolley, who financed construction of the Jolley Cut as a personal access road.
What is a "Cut"
The cliffs of The Escarpment (sometimes known as "Mount Hamilton") presents a major barrier to transportation. A cut is simply an incline cut into the face of the escarmpent to decrease the gradient and allow for easier travel. They are prone to damage by rockfalls and often have to be widened as demand increases. This photo shows Jolley cut in 1955.
The Paleozoic Platform
Between about 500 million years ago and 350 million years ago, central North America was a low-relief surface located variably at or below sea level. Shallow seas covered the continent and sedimentary rocks were deposited in layers upon the ancient Precambrian basement. This fairly flat-lying stack of rocks is known as the Paleozoic Platform.

Seismites
When sedimentary layers are subjected to earthquake activity while still soft, they may become deformed and contorted. In this case, a bed of sand collapsed into underlying layers to form a ball-and-pillow structure. We call any form generated by seismicity a "seismite".

Introduction
The city of Hamilton lies sprawled across the flat floor of former glacial Lake Iroquois, overlooked by the rim of the Niagara Escarpment, which forms the "Mountain" backdrop to the city. In several places, roads climb up the Escarpment face and cut through the vegetation to reveal the rock underneath.
The Electric City
In it's early days, Hamilton was known as the "Electric City". Hydroelectric power was harnessed at several spots along the Niagara Escarpment, notably from DeCew Falls, St. Catharines beginning in 1896. Cheap, efficient power attracted industry to the city and is partly responsible for the 75% population growth between 1901 and 1912.
Thorold Formation
The Thorold Formation was deposited in a sandy beach-like setting. During this time, the region was seismically active and many beds were deformed by the shaking.
Steel Central
Structures relating to steelmaking by companies like Stelco, and Dofasco can be seen in the distance. Hamilton became a centre for steelmaking in the 1910s due to the generation of cheap power off the Niagara Escarpment, it's central position within Great Lakes shipping routes, and availability of resources like limestone (in the escarpment) and coal (in Appalachia). The industry played a central role in the growth of Hamilton as a hub of economic activity.

Groundwater Seeps
Groundwater tends to travel fastest along the path of least resistance such as at the contact between two different types of rock or where rock is fractured. The place where it seeps to the surface tends to be marked by discolouration or vegetation.
The Attawandaron
This region was historically occupied by the Attawandaron, an Iroquoian-speaking, agricultural, village-dwelling society. European colonialism in the 17th century caused great social and economic disruption amongst the various First Nation groups of the Eastern Great Lakes, leading to widespread warfare. Though the Attawandaran attempted to stay neutral, and were even epitomized as the "Neutral Indians" by the French, they were dispersed around 1650 by conflict with the Haudenosaunee.
The Silurian of Ontario
During the Silurian Period, from 440-420 million years ago, Ontario lay to the west of the growing Taconic Mountains. Sediment shed from the mountains into a shallow seaway located on the interior of the continent. The depth of water and amount of sediment input fluctuated over time, resulting in the stratigraphy of shales, sandstones, limestones and dolostones that we see in the Niagara Escarpment.
