Springs Vs. Melt
Here we see the merging of two types of streams. The one closest to us is appears darker because it is more transparent "cleaner" water, while the larger stream it joins is more of a milky brown or blue from suspended glacial silts in the meltwater. The darker stream probably comes from a source that has been filtered as groundwater before coming to the surface as a spring.
Sunwapta Lake
Sunwapta Lake is a proglacial lake and temporary storage basin for glacial meltwaters. Glacial silt or 'rock flour' gives it a milky blue colour.
Ice Terminus
It takes about 200 years for ice to travel from the ice dome to the terminus of the glacier.
Recessional Moraines
These smaller mounds are linear recessional moraines formed during the 1900-1920s. This image shows Athabasca Glacier in 1906.
Dome Glacier
The Dome Glacier originates from three glaciers that spill off of bedrock cliffs and coelesce. It therefore has several medial moraines and a high degree of coverage by debris.
Washed Debris
Most of this debris on the ground has been washed of fine silts.
Ablation
Ablation is the loss of ice mass through sublimation, evaporation, and melting. Ablation gives the ice terminus a dirty appearance because the water dissapears while the sediment remains behind.
Lateral Moraine
This ridge is a lateral moraine. It forms from the collection of talus debris shed from valley walls on the lateral margins of flowing ice. The ice can then transport this debris downstream out onto the flat floor of the main valley.
Supraglacial Debris
Supraglacial debris collects on top of the ice. It is almost entirely talus which has fallen from steep valley walls and is usually quite angular.
Ice Dome
The Athabasca glacier descends about 800 m from the gentle dome of the Columbia Icefields, perched at an altitude of 2800 m.
Athabasca Glacier
Athabasca Glacier is a valley outlet glacier of the Columbia Icefield. It has a series of three icefalls as it descends from the Ice Dome into the valley.
Rilled Lateral Moraine
Although these enormous lateral moraines are only about 150 years old, they are already being degraded by running water carving deep rills into the surface.
Mount Athabasca
3491 m. Athabasca is the Cree Indian name for "where there are reeds" which originally referred to Lake Athabasca.
The Little Ice Age
The Little Ice Age was a period of globally cool temperatures that lasted from roughly 1350 to 1850. The Athabasca Glacier was at it's maximum as recently as 1843 when it created a large terminal moraine some 1.7 kilometres from it's current position.
Slumped Lateral Moraine
This part of the lateral moraine is starting to collapse under it's own weight.
Subglacial Debris
Subglacial debris is transported below the ice in a basal layer.
Striations
These scratches on the rock face are called striations. The were created by debris in the bottom of the ice sheet scratching the pavement.
Sunwapta to Athabasca
This is the Sunwapta River, a tributary of one of Canada's great heritage rivers: the Athabasca River. The Athabasca River drains the Rocky Mountains and flows over 1500 km across northern Alberta. It played an important role in the fur trade and in opening up western Canada.
Athabasca Retreat
The retreat rate as it has been observed over the past hundred years has not been constant. Between 1919 - 1948, it may have retreated at a rate of approx 20 m/yr, slowing to nearly stationary during the 70's. Today, the rate is around 5 m/year.
Equilibrium Line
The Equilibrium Line is where accumulation is equal to ablation. Most of what we can see here is below the equilibrium line, which means mass is being lost.
Terminal Moraine
The terminal moraine, also called an end moraine, marks the position of the ice margin at it's maximum extent. This ridge formed during the Little Ice Age.
