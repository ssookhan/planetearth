Throwing new light on old landscapes


Published 8/31/2021

by Nick Eyles

New mapping technologies using laser beams fired at the earth’s surface from above are transforming the way geologists see the Earth’s surface. In the 1920’s air photographs became part of the geologist’s arsenal. Then in the 1960’s satellite imagery emerged. However, the need to have ‘boots on the ground’ to field check observations never disappeared. Now, the emergence of super high-resolution LiDAR (Light imaging and Detection) creates images of earth’s surface of unparalleled definition well beyond anything that can be appreciated by eye from ground level where the geologist often cannot see the proverbial wood for the trees. This is akin to the improvements experienced when the first telescopes or microscopes opened new vistas of what cannot be directly observed by the naked eye.

How does it work?

Digital images are generated by recording the time it takes for laser beams to travel to the Earth’s surface and return to the transmitter. This produces datasets which capture the variations in elevation at the earth’s surface. As a result, data is often manipulated using automated machine learning techniques, to remove vegetation, even roads and buildings, to map the surface below.

How is LIDAR used to understand glacial landscapes?

LiDAR surveys in Canada are revealing the Ice Age history of landscapes formed under enormous continental ice sheets. These ice sheets scoured their beds as ice flowed from the source areas to the ice sheet margin many hundreds, and sometime thousands, of kilometers in the distance.

The use of LIDAR means that large areas can be mapped quickly, and with the assistance of drones mounted with LiDAR capable sensors formerly inaccessible areas can be mapped remotely in detail.

LIDAR datasets have been used in the lab to understand the classic glacial landform, the drumlin. These landforms were the subject of study and debate for more than 150 years. There are millions found across Canada as the product of erosion of older sediment that ice sheets overran as they grew in size, part of much larger streamlined surfaces that are akin to those found on faults, at the base of large landslides and on man-made surfaces (think brake pads) being worn by friction.

It turns out the drumlins are a part of a family of streamlined forms including deep groves and long ridges (called megascale glacial lineations). Studying these features is part of an emerging field called ‘glaciotribology’, the study of how surfaces were worn below large ice sheets. Understanding glacial movement is important for guiding the search for new mineral deposits where geologists and computer scientists are working together on the Canadian Shield to find the raw materials needed for a greener economy, and in turn, better understand how Canada’s glacial landscapes evolved.

How is LIDAR used by the Planet Earth Lab?

Recent work in the lab has demonstrated that the improved resolution of LIDAR data has allow for improved interpretation of the glacial landforms and the dynamics responsible for their formation. If you are interested in the lab’s recent use of high resolution LIDAR data see some examples in the the published articles below:
